Recent Projects
Interactive stories, in reverse chronological order. Tap the drawings for more info.

In the summer of 2016, I reached out to more than 200 people in a nationwide online survey that began with a simple question: "Do you remember where you were on New Year's Eve, 1999?" I also grabbed a microphone and hit the streets, talking to people from all walks of life about their thoughts and memories from the turn of the millennium. The result was this website, a hybrid work of data visualization, oral history and a longform journalism. It was my master's thesis at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill.

The CIA created the World Factbook as a classified document in 1962. They released it to the public in 1971, and it's now one of the leading sources of statistics on each of the world's 195 sovereign nations and 59 dependent territories. This website is a visualization of that data.

2016 U.S. Senate Elections
I built this website as a project at UNC. I had two goals: 1) Produce a work of data journalism with at least three visualizations; and 2) emulate the style of a major news publication.

To accomplish the first goal, I combed through U.S. census data and found some interesting trends in the migration patterns of Americans at different income levels. For the second objective, I chose to emulate the style of FiveThirtyEight (as it looked in fall 2015). The resemblance is purely for educational purposes.

When I was 23, I took a job as a private investigator in New York. It wasn’t what you think; I wasn’t spying on cheating spouses (at least not during work hours). It was corporate. I created dossiers on the people who sued companies for wrongful death and personal injury. My job, essentially, was to write life stories. Clear, concise, accurate, and on-a-tight-deadline life stories.

At first, I gathered this info by knocking on doors and flipping through dusty dockets in courthouse basements. Then the dockets went online, and so did the people (e.g., Facebook). I left the streets and opened a laptop. I wrote about everyone from arsonists to Nazi spies to Hall of Fame baseball players.

After a while, I realized these stories shouldn’t be crammed into the margins of a Word document. So I taught myself some front-end code and a few Adobe programs, then went to grad school to learn how to do it right.

Now, I can say — with a straight face — that I am an interactive multimedia investigative reporter. Most of my work is confidential. The apps in this portfolio are mostly personal projects, to give you a sense of how I roll, when allowed to roll freely.
